Chess Rulebook

Official rules, regulations, and guidelines for chess tournaments and competitions.

FIDE Laws of Chess

The FIDE Laws of Chess govern play on the chessboard. The Laws of Chess have two parts: 1. Basic Rules of Play and 2. Competition Rules. The English text is the authentic version of the Laws of Chess.

Below is a simplified version of the most important rules. For the complete official FIDE Laws of Chess, please download the PDF.

Article 1: The Nature and Objectives of Chess

Chess is played between two opponents who move pieces on a square board called a 'chessboard'. The player with the light-colored pieces (White) makes the first move, then players move alternately, with the player with the dark-colored pieces (Black) making the next move.

A player is said to 'have the move' when his opponent's move has been 'made'.

The objective of each player is to place the opponent's king 'under attack' in such a way that the opponent has no legal move. The player who achieves this goal is said to have 'checkmated' the opponent's king and to have won the game. The opponent whose king has been checkmated has lost the game.

If the position is such that neither player can possibly checkmate the opponent's king, the game is drawn.

Article 2: The Initial Position of the Pieces on the Chessboard

The chessboard is composed of an 8 x 8 grid of 64 equal squares alternately light (the 'white' squares) and dark (the 'black' squares).

The chessboard is placed between the players in such a way that the near corner square to the right of the player is white.

At the beginning of the game, White has 16 light-colored pieces; Black has 16 dark-colored pieces:

  • A white king, usually indicated by the symbol ♔
  • A white queen, usually indicated by the symbol ♕
  • Two white rooks, usually indicated by the symbol ♖
  • Two white bishops, usually indicated by the symbol ♗
  • Two white knights, usually indicated by the symbol ♘
  • Eight white pawns, usually indicated by the symbol ♙
  • A black king, usually indicated by the symbol ♚
  • A black queen, usually indicated by the symbol ♛
  • Two black rooks, usually indicated by the symbol ♜
  • Two black bishops, usually indicated by the symbol ♝
  • Two black knights, usually indicated by the symbol ♞
  • Eight black pawns, usually indicated by the symbol ♟

Article 3: The Moves of the Pieces

It is not permitted to move a piece to a square occupied by a piece of the same color. If a piece moves to a square occupied by an opponent's piece the latter is captured and removed from the chessboard as part of the same move.

The King

The king moves one square in any direction (horizontal, vertical, or diagonal).

There is a special move called castling that involves both the king and one rook.

The Queen

The queen moves to any square along the file, the rank, or a diagonal on which it stands.

The Rook

The rook moves to any square along the file or the rank on which it stands.

The Bishop

The bishop moves to any square along a diagonal on which it stands.

The Knight

The knight moves to one of the squares nearest to that on which it stands but not on the same rank, file, or diagonal. The knight is the only piece that can leap over other pieces.

The Pawn

The pawn moves forward one square, but on its first move it can move forward two squares. The pawn captures diagonally forward one square.

When a pawn reaches the rank furthest from its starting position it must be exchanged as part of the same move for a new queen, rook, bishop, or knight of the same color. This is called 'promotion'.

En Passant

A pawn attacking a square crossed by an opponent's pawn which has advanced two squares in one move from its original square may capture this opponent's pawn as though the latter had been moved only one square. This capture is only legal on the move following this advance.

Castling

Castling is a move of the king and either rook of the same color along the player's first rank, counting as a single move of the king and executed as follows: the king is transferred from its original square two squares towards the rook on its original square, then that rook is transferred to the square the king has just crossed.

Castling is prevented temporarily:

  • if the square on which the king stands, or the square which it must cross, or the square which it is to occupy, is attacked by one or more of the opponent's pieces, or
  • if there is any piece between the king and the rook with which castling is to be effected.

Article 4: The Act of Moving the Pieces

Each move must be made with one hand only. When castling, the player must first move the king with one hand and then move the rook with the same hand.

Provided that he first expresses his intention (for example by saying "j'adoube" or "I adjust"), only the player having the move may adjust one or more pieces on their squares.

When a player touches one of his pieces with the intention of moving it, he is obliged to move it if it can be moved. When a player touches one of his opponent's pieces with the intention of capturing it, he is obliged to capture it if it can be captured.

Article 5: The Completion of the Game

The game is won by the player who has checkmated his opponent's king.

The game is won by the player whose opponent declares he resigns. This immediately ends the game.

The game is drawn when the player to move has no legal move and his king is not in check. The game is said to end in 'stalemate'. This immediately ends the game.

The game is drawn when a position has arisen in which neither player can checkmate the opponent's king with any series of legal moves. The game is said to end in a 'dead position'. This immediately ends the game.

The game is drawn upon agreement between the two players during the game. This immediately ends the game.

The game may be drawn if an identical position is about to appear or has appeared on the chessboard at least three times.

The game may be drawn if each player has made at least the last 50 consecutive moves without the movement of any pawn and without any capture.

Tournament Regulations

The following regulations apply to all tournaments organized by the Haryana Chess Association. These regulations are in addition to the FIDE Laws of Chess and may be modified for specific tournaments.

Time Controls

Standard tournaments: 90 minutes for the first 40 moves, followed by 30 minutes for the rest of the game with an addition of 30 seconds per move starting from move one.

Rapid tournaments: 15 minutes for the entire game with an addition of 10 seconds per move starting from move one.

Blitz tournaments: 3 minutes for the entire game with an addition of 2 seconds per move starting from move one.

Eligibility

All players must be registered with the Haryana Chess Association and have a valid HCA ID.

For age-restricted tournaments, players must provide proof of age.

For rated tournaments, players must have a valid AICF registration.

Tie-Breaks

In case of a tie, the following tie-break systems will be applied in the given order:

  1. Direct Encounter
  2. Buchholz Cut 1
  3. Buchholz
  4. Sonneborn-Berger
  5. Greater number of wins

Anti-Cheating

Players are not allowed to bring electronic devices into the playing venue without prior permission from the arbiter.

Random checks may be conducted using metal detectors.

The playing venue may be under CCTV surveillance.

Violation of anti-cheating rules may result in immediate disqualification and further disciplinary action.

Code of Conduct

All players, coaches, officials, and spectators are expected to adhere to the following code of conduct during chess events organized by the Haryana Chess Association:

Sportsmanship

Demonstrate good sportsmanship at all times. Shake hands before and after the game. Accept defeat gracefully and victory modestly.

Silence

Maintain silence in the playing hall. Do not disturb your opponent or other players. Turn off all electronic devices.

Respect for Officials

Respect the decisions of arbiters and tournament officials. If you disagree with a decision, follow the proper appeal procedure.

Fair Play

Play fairly and honestly. Do not use or attempt to use any external assistance during your games. Report any suspicious behavior to the arbiter.

Punctuality

Arrive on time for your games. Players who arrive after the default time (usually 30 minutes) will forfeit the game.

Respect for All

Treat all players, coaches, officials, and spectators with respect regardless of age, gender, race, religion, or ability.

Violation of the code of conduct may result in penalties ranging from warnings to disqualification from the tournament and suspension from future events.
